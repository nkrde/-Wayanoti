02 — What Users Experienced

What users experienced

Android

“My daughter set the font size bigger on my phone. Now most of the trade message gets cut off, so I have to open the app to see what's actually being recommended.”

iOS

“Most of the time I'm just glancing at my phone between meetings. If I have to stop and think about what the notification means, it's already too much effort.”

Why this mattered

Waya's trade recommendations are time-sensitive. A strike price, entry range, or stop-loss can lose relevance within minutes. When users struggle to read or understand a notification, the issue isn't just usability—it directly affects their ability to act on a trade opportunity.

03 — Context

The notification is not a marketing message. It's the interface between research and money moving.

Waya runs advisory across four products — Equity, FnO, MCX, and PMS — each governed by SEBI compliance. Subscribers act on signals that are correct for minutes, not hours: a strike price, an entry range, a stop loss.

Every signal travels the same path. The card is the bottleneck in that chain — if it's slow, wrong, or unclear, the trade is functionally dead before it reaches the user.

04 — The problem, and what it cost

Three failures that showed up together, because they shared one root cause.

Before this system existed, every trade card was built by hand — in Canva, under time pressure, by whoever was on shift. Speed, accuracy, and consistency broke at the same time, because all three were racing the same closing window.

05 — How the problem was found

The first step wasn't design. It was an audit.

I pulled live notifications from Waya FnO and Waya MCX across real devices — iOS, and Android at both standard and large system font sizes — and laid them against competitor advisory apps, Univest and Multyfi, to see how the same kind of time-sensitive information was being handled elsewhere.

In parallel, I mapped the complete FNO and MCX user journey — registration through subscription through signal delivery through execution — to see exactly where the card sits in that cycle, and how much time pressure surrounds that one step relative to everything around it.

06 — From research to rules

The audit produced constraints, not a style guide.

This is the bridge between research and the tool — the rules the tool would later be built to enforce automatically.

01
Word limit per line Calibrated against the worst-case Android large-font rendering, not the average case.
02
Image and text hierarchy The single most decision-relevant fact — strike, entry, stop loss — always renders in the first visible line.
03
A font-size ceiling Past a certain system font size, copy doesn't shrink — it truncates. The structure assumes the largest setting, not the average one.

07 — The AI-built solution

Waya Studio: a rules engine, not a copywriter.

The rules existed on paper. The next problem was getting someone working fast to follow them every time, without having to remember them.

It's worth being precise about what AI means here. Waya Studio does not generate copy with AI. It's a structured interface — the AI tooling (Google AI Studio, Gemini) was used to build the system itself, not to write the words inside it.

The tool branches by product, because each one carries a different field set.

The result: a designer or ops team member fills structured fields — they don't design a layout from scratch. The card that comes out the other side is correct by construction. It cannot violate the rules from section 06, because the interface doesn't expose the freedom to violate them.

08 — UX heuristics this was built on

Four heuristics, each tied to one real decision.

Error prevention

The schema doesn't validate input after the fact — it removes the invalid state from existing in the first place. A strike price can't be typed into a stop-loss field, because FNO and MCX don't share a layout.

Consistency and standards

One brand system, enforced identically across three products that previously had three different informal conventions depending on who built the last card.

Recognition over recall

Ops doesn't need to remember the font-size ceiling or the word limit. The fields are short by design — there's no room to type past the limit.

Visibility of system status

The live preview updates with every field change, at the real export size. What's on screen before export is what ships — no blind exports.

11 · Future Explorable Processes

Designing for the Next Phase of AI-Driven Interfaces

Future design iterations can explore several critical patterns at the intersection of AI advisory logic and automated notifications:

Prompt UX
Designing how users communicate with AI to generate and refine alerts.
Agentic UX
AI completing entire end-to-end task workflows instead of simply responding to prompts.
Multimodal UX
Creating a unified, seamless experience spanning text, voice, images, and raw documents.
Generative UI
Interfaces that adapt their layouts, widgets, and elements dynamically to specific users and contexts.
Human in the Loop
AI systems speed up decisions and generate structures while human operators remain in final control.
Intent-Based UX
Designing interfaces entirely around high-level user goals rather than low-level system inputs.
